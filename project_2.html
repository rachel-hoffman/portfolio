User Research for a Website Redesign

A Team-Based Research Project


Background

This project put me into a group of five other UX students, and together we were tasked with looking into possible problem areas for users of the Michigan State University Writing Center website. Both students and employees at the writing center had reported having issues with the appointment-making process on the site. This issue was explained to us by Trixie Smith, the Writing Center’s Director. She saw that the actual coding of the site worked just fine, but it was the connection between software and user which was failing. Me and my team were tasked with gathering and analyzing user feedback on the current site, creating a mockup design that incorporated elements based on that feedback, and presenting our findings to Dr. Smith.

Narrowing our Focus

Our team first conducted user testing with the current system using ourselves as the user. While this does introduce bias, with the time constraints we were working with it made the most sense since we only had time for one round of public data collection and design ideation for this project. This initial process allowed us to have a baseline of understanding of the current system for ourselves. It was my role within the team to document our process. At this stage, I kept track of our initial impressions of the website and what we thought the possible problem areas could be. This gave us a jumping-off point for where to focus our future research.

Onto Testing

My team and I went into our data gathering hoping to reach as large and diverse a group of people as we could within the given time period. This led us to developing an online survey through Google Forms. This made it easy to spread around to people through the use of a simple scan-and-go QR code. We were able to post it around the MSU campus so that we got varying types of users, as we wanted to avoid gathering data on just one demographic of users which could heavily bias the data.

The survey data gave us an overview of our user demographic and needs; however my team and I wanted to further our research before moving on to our design ideation. To complement this broad, quantitative data gathered, we decided to conduct user interviews to add on more specific, qualitative data. We had two team members put together a case study on one individual’s experience with the site. Using this testing method, we were able to get descriptive feedback on what problem areas there were and what design choices the user was expecting to see.

Design Ideation

Putting together the data gathered, my team and I saw that most users were struggling to understand and navigate the appointment-scheduling page of the website. It was quickly agreed that we needed to focus our attention on redesigning the scheduling page, whose coloring and layout was proven to not be user-friendly to newcomers. We compiled a list of changes we wanted to make, and I was given the role of making our mockup and then our prototype design.

I ended up creating a clickable prototype in Google Slides that allowed the client to see how the final version would work. My team and I had found that the flow of the site did not match the presumed process in the user’s mind. I wanted to make the scheduling site much simpler. I made its use more straight-forward for users by having a clear flow guiding them through the process and not throwing too much irrelevant information at them. That is why I have the information organized section-by-section, and it is revealed as users work through selecting their preferences. I also wanted to make a green and white themed site design, unlike the gray and blue theme that felt out-of-place for an MSU site and did not match the Writing Center homepage.
